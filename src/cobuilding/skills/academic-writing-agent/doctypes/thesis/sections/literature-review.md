# Literature Review Chapter Conventions

A thesis literature review is the candidate's demonstration that they understand the field. Unlike a paper introduction, it goes broader, deeper, and slower. Committees read it to assess scholarship; reviewers of a defense remember it as the thing that convinced them the candidate can hold their own with the field's experts.

## Principles

1. **Synthesize, do not summarize.** A literature review is not a sequence of paper summaries. It is the candidate's argument about how the field arrived at its current understanding, organized around themes, debates, or theoretical lineages — not author by author or year by year.

2. **Show the seams.** Where the field disagrees, name the disagreement and the sides. Where evidence is thin, say so. Where methodological assumptions differ, surface them. A literature review that reads as smooth consensus is suspicious; real fields have controversies.

3. **Position the dissertation at the end.** The literature review's job is twofold: demonstrate scholarship, and motivate the dissertation. The chapter closes by pointing to the gap or question that the rest of the thesis addresses, and how. This is the bridge to Chapter 2.

4. **Coverage matters; selectivity matters more.** Cite the foundational and recent work, but skip work that does not contribute to the argument. Committees notice padding ("the candidate clearly Googled rather than read") and gaps ("the candidate missed Smith 2019, the most relevant paper in the field"). Both are penalties.

5. **Organize by theme, debate, or theoretical lineage.** Possible structures: chronological (when the field's history matters), thematic (most common), debate-oriented (when controversies define the field), methodological (when techniques shape what is knowable). Pick one and stick with it; do not switch structures mid-chapter.

6. **Use sub-headers liberally.** Literature reviews are long; sub-headers help the reader navigate. Two or three levels of sub-headers is normal.

7. **Length is a function of field and committee expectations.** Some departments expect 20 pages, some 60. The defense-driving question is whether the chapter equips the reader to evaluate the rest of the dissertation. If yes, length is right.

8. **Citations are dense and synthesized.** A claim with one citation often signals shallow reading; a claim with five citations across three theoretical positions signals depth. But avoid citation soup — group references to signal the synthesis, do not list them individually unless that adds something.

## Common Failure Modes

- **Reads as annotated bibliography.** Paragraph 1 is about Smith 2015. Paragraph 2 is about Jones 2017. Paragraph 3 is about Lee 2019. No argument; just summaries.
- **Field-wide overview with no link to the dissertation.** Closes Chapter 1 without pointing to Chapter 2; reader is left wondering why the chapter was here.
- **Echoes of seminar talks.** "An important paper was X. Another important paper was Y." Writing for an audience that has heard the seminar version. Replace with synthesis.
- **Missing recent work.** Citations end three years before the dissertation date. Committees check the most recent year explicitly.
- **Missing competing views.** The chapter presents one school's interpretation as if it were consensus. Acknowledge alternatives.
- **Methodological assumptions invisible.** The literature is summarized as a unified body when the field actually has methodological camps that disagree about what counts as evidence. Surface this.

## Citations

The heaviest section of the dissertation for citations. Engage with the original sources, not just secondary syntheses. When the candidate references prior work not already in their library, route through Cite for verification before suggesting it — fabricated or misattributed references in a literature review are catastrophic if a committee member catches them.

## When the manuscript is in early drafting

If the chapter is bulleted or in section-header-only form, treat it as Outline (per `doctypes/academic-paper/sections/outline.md`) but with thesis-scale expectations: comment on the organizing structure (chronological vs. thematic), the apparent breadth, and whether the candidate has identified the right themes for the dissertation's question.
