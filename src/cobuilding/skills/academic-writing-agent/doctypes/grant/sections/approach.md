# Approach Section Conventions

Approach is the longest and most technically detailed section of an NIH grant. It is where reviewers test whether the author can actually do what the Aims promise. Most fundable critiques live in Approach.

## Principles

1. **Organize by Aim.** Approach mirrors the Aims structure. Each aim gets a sub-section. Within each, follow a consistent internal order — rationale, preliminary data, experimental design, expected outcomes, alternative strategies, pitfalls. Reviewers learn the structure quickly and look for missing elements.

2. **Preliminary data placement.** Each Aim should open with preliminary data that demonstrates feasibility. Preliminary data is the single strongest evidence the author can actually execute the proposed work. Without it, the Aim looks aspirational. With it, the Aim looks de-risked.

3. **Rigor and reproducibility, explicitly.** NIH requires explicit discussion of rigor in design (controls, blinding, randomization where applicable, biological replicates, technical replicates), authentication of key resources (cell lines, antibodies), and consideration of relevant biological variables (sex, age, strain). Do not bury these — call them out.

4. **Sample sizes and statistical plan.** State n per group, effect size assumed, power calculation, and the statistical test. Reviewers check this; vague statistics ("appropriate tests will be applied") draw flags.

5. **Expected outcomes.** What does the author expect to find? Specifying expected outcomes is not over-claiming — it shows the author has thought through the experiments. Reviewers prefer specific predictions to vague hopes.

6. **Alternative strategies and contingencies.** What if Aim 1's central experiment fails? What is plan B? Reviewers love seeing this. It signals that the author has stress-tested the proposal. Every Aim should have at least one contingency.

7. **Timeline.** A simple Gantt-style chart or a one-paragraph timeline by year shows the work fits the funding period. R01s typically span 4–5 years; aims should overlap in time, not run strictly sequentially.

8. **Vertebrate Animals, Human Subjects, Biohazards.** If applicable, address them in dedicated sub-sections following NIH formatting. The grant will be returned without review if these are missing.

## Common Failure Modes

- **Aims that are too dependent.** If Aim 2 requires Aim 1 to succeed, reviewers worry the whole grant is one experiment. Re-architect to make aims independently fundable.
- **No alternatives.** Aim 1 will work or the grant has no path forward. Reviewers will mark this as "high risk."
- **Buried preliminary data.** Preliminary data should be the first paragraph of each Aim, not the last.
- **Methods without rationale.** A two-paragraph block of protocol with no explanation of why this protocol fits the question reads like a methods section, not an argument.
- **No rigor section.** Even if rigor is woven through, an explicit "Rigor of experimental design" sub-section is expected.
- **No biological variables.** Even non-sex-relevant proposals should explicitly address why sex is or is not relevant to the question.

## Citations

Heavy in early paragraphs (where the author argues why a chosen method is the right one) and in alternative strategies (citing precedent for the fallback approach). Route any new references through Cite before adding.

## When the author has not provided preliminary data

If the manuscript lacks preliminary data, name the gap explicitly and suggest what kind of preliminary data would strengthen each Aim. Do not invent preliminary data. The author may also be in early drafting, in which case the agent should treat Approach as Outline (per the academic-paper Outline conventions) and focus on the plan rather than missing prose.
