# Innovation Section Conventions

Innovation answers "what is new about this approach?" — and crucially, "why is that newness needed to make progress?" Often the shortest section in a grant, but its absence or weakness gets called out in review.

## Principles

1. **Innovation is contrast.** Innovation is not "we are doing something good." It is "the field currently does X; we will do Y, which is different in these specific ways and enables specific new outcomes." Always frame as contrast to current state-of-the-art.

2. **Three flavors of innovation, name yours.** Concept (a new hypothesis or theoretical framing), method (a new technique, assay, or analysis), or application (applying an existing tool to a problem where it has not been applied). Most proposals have one or two; some have all three. Be explicit about which.

3. **Avoid "novel" and "innovative" as labels.** Tell the reviewer what is new and why it matters; do not just claim novelty. "We will use single-molecule FRET in living cells, which has not been demonstrated for proteins of this size" is innovation; "we present a novel and innovative approach" is filler.

4. **Tie innovation to feasibility.** A reviewer's reflexive concern about innovation is risk. Acknowledge what is unproven, and point to preliminary data or analogous published work that suggests the innovation will work.

5. **Distinguish from competitors.** If a few other groups are pursuing similar approaches, acknowledge them and explain what is distinctive about this proposal. Reviewers will know the competing approaches; failing to acknowledge them looks naive.

6. **Short is fine.** Innovation can be half a page or less. Do not pad to fill space; reviewers respect concision here more than in other sections.

## Common Failure Modes

- **Claiming innovation without demonstrating it.** Words like "transformative" without specifics signal weakness.
- **Innovation that is really Approach.** If a paragraph describes how an experiment will be done, it belongs in Approach. Innovation is the strategic-level argument for why this approach.
- **Innovation that contradicts Significance.** If Significance argues the problem is widely studied and Innovation claims the proposal is the only one tackling it, reviewers will catch the contradiction.

## Citations

Lighter citation than Significance. Cite primarily to support contrast claims ("Current methods rely on X [refs]; we propose Y") and to acknowledge competing approaches ("Group Z has pursued a related strategy [ref]; our approach differs in [specifics]").
