# Specific Aims Section Conventions

The Specific Aims page is the most-read part of an NIH grant. Many reviewers form their initial assessment from this page alone. Every sentence carries reviewer attention; every word competes for space.

## Principles

1. **One page, hard limit.** NIH R01 is one page; some mechanisms allow more. The page limit is the most-violated constraint reviewers see — always treat it as binding. Cut content, do not shrink fonts or margins.

2. **Opening paragraph: hook, gap, payoff.** Open with a problem statement that the reviewer will recognize as important. State the gap in current knowledge or capability. Close the opener with what this proposal will deliver — the "payoff" the rest of the page argues for.

3. **State a long-term goal and a central hypothesis.** The long-term goal frames career trajectory; the central hypothesis frames this proposal's logic. Both should be one sentence each. The hypothesis must be testable and the aims must follow from it.

4. **Two or three aims, not four or five.** Each aim has a one-sentence statement (often italicized or bolded), a brief rationale, and a one-to-two-sentence approach summary. Reviewers expect aims to be independent — a failure in Aim 1 should not torpedo Aim 2.

5. **Aim structure is non-arbitrary.** Either parallel ("Aim 1: determine X. Aim 2: determine Y. Aim 3: determine Z.") or building ("Aim 1 establishes the system. Aim 2 perturbs it. Aim 3 applies the perturbation.") — but explicitly one or the other. Never both. If aims feel mixed, restructure.

6. **Avoid descriptive aims.** "Characterize X" is a weak aim. "Determine whether X is required for Y by performing Z" is a strong aim — it commits to a question and an approach.

7. **Close with impact, not summary.** The final paragraph should state the expected outcome and its impact on the field, the agency's mission, or public health. Do not restate the aims.

8. **No filler, no preamble.** Every sentence earns its place. "Background" paragraphs do not belong on the aims page — that's what Significance is for.

## Common Failure Modes

- **Hypothesis missing or buried.** Reviewers should be able to point to a single sentence and say "this is the hypothesis."
- **Aims that are projects rather than questions.** "Aim 1: develop a method" — what will the method tell us? Add a question.
- **Aims that depend on each other.** If Aim 2 cannot start until Aim 1 succeeds, restructure.
- **Page-limit creep.** Lines that span seven words across the page exist to be cut, not preserved.

## Citations

The Aims page typically includes a small number of citations — only the most load-bearing references to support the gap statement and the rationale. Do not pad with citations on the Aims page; citations belong in Significance and Approach.
