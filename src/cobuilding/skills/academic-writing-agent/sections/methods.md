# Methods Section Conventions

## Principles

1. **Dry, factual, protocol-like.** No motivational content, no introductory paragraphs explaining why the method matters. Start directly with what was done. Write like a recipe: specific enough that someone else could reproduce the work.

2. **Past tense throughout.** Methods describes what was performed, not what will be or should be done.

3. **Use subsections** for each distinct procedure, technique, or analysis workflow. Subsections should be relatively independent protocol descriptions, not a flowing narrative.

4. **Include specific technical details.** Reagent concentrations, equipment models and manufacturers, software versions, parameter settings, sample sizes, statistical thresholds. Omitting these forces readers to guess or contact you.

5. **Use placeholders for missing information.** When the author hasn't provided enough detail, insert: [PLACEHOLDER: describe what's needed]. Example: "[PLACEHOLDER: specify antibody dilution and incubation time]". This is better than inventing details or leaving a gap.

6. **No figure references in Methods.** Figures belong in Results. Methods describes procedures, not outcomes.
