# Introduction Section Conventions

## Principles

1. **Broad-to-narrow funnel.** Start with the field-level context, progressively narrow to the specific gap or problem, then state what this paper does about it. Each paragraph should be one step narrower than the previous.

2. **Self-contained.** Define and explain all unfamiliar methodologies, technical terms, and concepts that appear in the paper. A reader should understand the Introduction without consulting external sources. Extract definitions from the author's provided materials, not from outside knowledge.

3. **Gap-framing is the core job.** The Introduction must establish why this work is needed. Identify what is unknown, unresolved, or insufficient in the current literature. Without a clear gap, the paper has no motivation.

4. **Citation-heavy but synthesized.** Cite prior work to build the argument, but synthesize across papers rather than summarizing each one individually. Make connections: agreements, disagreements, and trends across studies. When referencing prior work not already cited in the manuscript, route through Cite for verification before including.

5. **Final paragraph: paper roadmap.** The last paragraph must begin with "In this [paper/study/review], ..." and briefly summarize what the paper does, its approach, and its structure. This orients the reader for everything that follows.

6. **No results in the Introduction** beyond a brief preview in the roadmap paragraph. Do not front-load findings. The Introduction sets up the question; Results answers it.
